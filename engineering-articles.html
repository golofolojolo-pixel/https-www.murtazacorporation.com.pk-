PHMSA Issues Multiple Rulemakings to Update the Federal Pipeline Safety Regulations

On April 24, 2026, the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration issued 40 regulatory updates amending parts 191, 192, and 195 of the federal pipeline safety regulations. These rulemakings affect operators of gas, hazardous liquid, carbon dioxide, underground natural gas storage, and liquefied natural gas facilities. Public comments are due by June 23, 2026, with most final rules taking effect on August 3, 2026.

Proposed changes include extending annual report filing deadlines, raising the monetary thresholds that trigger construction notifications, eliminating outdated welding limitations, and extending mainline valve inspection intervals for hazardous liquid pipelines to a maximum of 15 months. Operators would be permitted to use remote sensing technologies for right-of-way patrols and to remotely monitor rectifiers. The proposals also raise the property damage threshold for reporting releases, eliminate redundant material verification during pressure reconfirmations, and increase the operational timeframe for newly installed rupture-mitigation valves from 14 to 90 days. Separate direct final rules clarify hazardous liquid pipeline integrity management, allow electronic response plan storage, and incorporate 15 updated industry standards; any direct final rule receiving adverse comment by the deadline will be withdrawn and republished as a proposed rulemaking.

J.F. Lehman's Forged Solutions Acquires Custom Alloy

J.F. Lehman & Company portfolio company Forged Solutions Group has acquired Custom Alloy Corporation, realigning it within the J.F. Lehman portfolio following its 2023 acquisition by Trident Maritime Systems during a bankruptcy process. Forged Solutions Group specializes in high-specification forged metal components — rolled ring, closed-die, open-die, and extrusion forgings — for aerospace, defense, and space applications.

Custom Alloy is a vertically integrated manufacturer producing open- and closed-die forgings along with seamless and welded pipe fittings for high-pressure, corrosive, and nuclear environments. It holds Level 1 qualifications from the U.S. Navy as both a forging and fitting manufacturer — one of only three national companies with both designations — and serves as sole-source manufacturer for Columbia-class Main Seawater Fittings and a dual-source supplier for Virginia-class Main Seawater Fittings. The acquisition gives Forged Solutions Group critical exposure to the U.S. nuclear navy supply chain, positioning the combined entity to address shipbuilding supply chain bottlenecks tied to Navy submarine programs and the AUKUS partnership. Custom Alloy operates four manufacturing facilities totaling 450,000 square feet near Allentown in High Bridge, New Jersey.

304 vs 316 Stainless Steel: Choosing the Right Grade

304 and 316 are the two grades that come up in almost every piping enquiry, and it's easy to see why they get confused — both are austenitic, both are non-magnetic in the annealed condition, and both machine, weld and polish in much the same way. The difference that matters is molybdenum: 316 contains 2–3% Mo, which 304 does not, and that single alloying addition changes where each grade belongs in a plant.

Where 304 is the right call

304 (and its low-carbon version, 304L, for welded fabrication) handles general atmospheric exposure, freshwater, most food contact, and a wide range of mild chemical environments comfortably. It's the more economical of the two grades, and for piping, fittings and tanks that never see chlorides in any concentration, specifying 316 is usually money spent without a return.

Where 316 earns its premium

Molybdenum sharply improves resistance to pitting and crevice corrosion in chloride-bearing environments — seawater, brine, coastal atmospheres, de-icing salts, and many pharmaceutical and chemical process streams. If a line will regularly see chlorides above a few hundred ppm, or the application is offshore, marine, or coastal, 316 (or 316L) is the safer default. Dairy and beverage lines that undergo frequent CIP cleaning with chloride-based sanitizers are a common case where 316L is specified even though the product itself is benign — see our dairy tube & fitting range for the hygienic-finish options usually paired with it.

A quick decision checklist

  • Freshwater, dry atmosphere, general indoor process — 304/304L is normally sufficient.
  • Coastal, marine, or chloride-cleaned service — step up to 316/316L.
  • Strong acids, high-chloride brines, or sour service — 316 may still not be enough; ask about duplex or super-austenitic grades.
  • When in doubt, check the actual chloride concentration and operating temperature of the media, not just its name — "salt" and "saline" cover a huge range of severity.

Both grades are stocked across our pipe, fittings and tube ranges — talk to us with your media, temperature and cleaning regime and we'll confirm the grade before you order.

Pipe Schedules Explained: Picking the Right Wall Thickness

The "schedule" number stamped on a pipe spec — SCH10, SCH40, SCH80, SCH160 — is simply a standardized way of expressing wall thickness relative to outside diameter, as set out in ASME B36.19 (stainless) and B36.10 (carbon steel). A higher schedule number means a thicker wall for the same nominal pipe size (NPS/DN), and therefore a smaller internal bore.

What actually drives the schedule choice

Wall thickness selection comes down to four factors working together, not any one number in isolation:

  • Design pressure — the primary driver; higher internal pressure needs more wall thickness at a given diameter.
  • Design temperature — allowable stress for the material drops as temperature rises, so the same pressure needs a thicker wall at higher operating temperature.
  • Corrosion/erosion allowance — an extra margin of thickness added so the pipe still meets minimum requirements after years of service wear.
  • Joint efficiency and manufacturing method — seamless pipe and welded pipe are rated differently under the same code because of how the weld seam is accounted for.

Common schedules in practice

SCH10 and SCH10S are common in lower-pressure stainless process lines where cost and weight matter. SCH40 (and 40S) is the general-purpose default across a huge range of industrial piping. SCH80 and SCH160 step up for higher-pressure or higher-temperature service, and XXS covers the most demanding cases. The "S" suffix specifically denotes the stainless steel series dimensions under B36.19, which don't always match the carbon steel series at the same schedule number — worth checking before assuming a schedule carries over between materials.

A practical note for buyers

The schedule on a data sheet should always be checked against the actual design pressure/temperature calculation for the line — never assumed from a similar-looking project. Quality & Traceability Jul 2026·5 min read

Reading a Mill Test Certificate: 3.1 vs 3.2 Explained

Almost every purchase order for pipe, fittings or valves asks for a "Mill Test Certificate" or "MTC," usually with a reference to EN 10204. In practice, the standard defines several inspection document types, and mixing them up can mean receiving less traceability than a project actually needs.

Type 2.2 — a manufacturer's declaration

A 2.2 report is issued by the manufacturer based on non-specific inspection — it confirms the product complies with the order, but the test results shown may come from routine production testing rather than the specific batch supplied. It's adequate for lower-criticality, non-pressure applications.

Type 3.1 — validated by the manufacturer

A 3.1 certificate is issued and validated by the manufacturer's own authorized quality department, independent of the production department, and the results shown are specific to the material actually supplied. This is the standard requirement for most pressure piping, valves and fittings in industrial and process plant work, and is what our Mill Test Report documentation is issued to.

Type 3.2 — validated by an independent third party

A 3.2 certificate carries everything a 3.1 does, but is additionally validated either by an inspector designated by the purchaser or by an inspector responsible for compliance with the official regulations, independent of both manufacturer and purchaser. This is typically reserved for safety-critical or code-mandated projects — pressure vessels, nuclear, or client specifications that explicitly call for third-party witness — and comes with a corresponding lead time and cost.

What to check before you specify one

  • Confirm whether your project spec, client, or governing code actually mandates 3.2 — many projects default to asking for it when 3.1 would satisfy the requirement, adding cost and lead time unnecessarily.
  • Check that heat/batch numbers on the certificate match the physical markings on the pipe, fitting or valve received — traceability is only useful if it's verifiable on-site.
  • For sanitary and dairy lines, ask whether the certificate covers surface finish and passivation results in addition to chemical composition and mechanical properties.
